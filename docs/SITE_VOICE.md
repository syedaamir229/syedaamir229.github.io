# Site Voice

Voice rules for landing-surface copy on syedaamir229.github.io: the home page, capability cards, project list descriptions, section headers, CTAs, and the experience page.

For long-form blog posts, see `BLOG_VOICE.md`. The two are aligned but address different surfaces. This file is the shorter, tighter spec for everything that is not a blog post.

---

## The one rule

**Lead with what changed for the business. The engineering verb moves to the detail page, not the landing surface.**

A reader scanning the home page or a project list should see, in plain language, what is now true that was not true before. Past-tense CV phrasing ("built", "designed", "stood up", "contributed to", "delivered") belongs inside the case study, not on the card that links to it.

## What this looks like in practice

### Headlines (already good, keep)

- "Data that works as hard as your business does."
- "End KPI disputes and make reporting self-serve."
- "Turning scattered data into decisions your business can trust."

Headlines speak to a buyer in their language. Keep them direct, present-tense, outcome-shaped.

### Bullets on capability cards

Two-tier: a short label (2-3 words) and one sentence in plain business language under it.

Good:
- **Unified data model.** CRM, billing, product, and content stitched into a single picture of the business.

Bad (CV voice):
- Design unified data models from multiple source systems.

### Project descriptions (the one-line summary on cards)

Present-tense outcome, not past-tense delivery. Same facts, different center of gravity.

Good:
- "Standardized reporting across departments so leadership stopped reconciling spreadsheets."

Bad (CV voice):
- "Contributed to a multi-department BI rollout that standardized reporting across business units."

### Section subheadings and frame lines

Avoid quietly resume-style framing. The reader is not buying a person; they are buying an outcome.

Good:
- "Where this approach has paid off, across data platforms, analytics, and AI."

Bad:
- "Selected projects from 10 years of delivery across data platforms, analytics, and AI automation."

## Vocabulary to avoid on landing surfaces

These words drag the tone back to CV mode. Use them in the detail body, not the card.

- built, designed, developed, delivered, implemented, deployed
- stood up, contributed to, led, drove, owned, championed
- "leveraging", "utilizing", "comprehensive", "robust", "scalable" (as adjectives without a specific number behind them)

## Vocabulary to prefer

Words a CFO, COO, or product VP would say out loud.

- "agree on", "trust", "see", "act on", "stop", "no longer", "instead of"
- concrete business nouns: revenue, churn, campaigns, dashboards, reports, customers, segments
- "in one place", "in one click", "without breaking", "before anyone touches it"

## Vertical agnosticism on the landing surfaces

The Capability cards, hero, and section subheads are pitched to any buyer: media, banking, retail, government, B2B SaaS. Avoid words that quietly assume one vertical.

Pull these out of landing copy and put them in the project case studies, where they earn their keep as proof:

- Subscription/SaaS-flavored: subscribers, viewers, episodes, churn, retention, MRR, ARR
- Region-flavored: Arabic, MENA, Dubai, UAE (the experience timeline already establishes the location; cards do not need to repeat it)
- Tool-flavored: Databricks, Power BI, Tableau, CleverTap (named tools belong in project tags and detail bodies)

Use universal roles and entities instead: customers, transactions, operations, finance, marketing, sales, risk, intent, records, content, accounts. The Pipeline section ("How I Work") is the model for tone here, since it already does this well.

Project descriptions and case studies are the opposite: keep them vertical-specific. They are evidence, not a pitch.

## Mechanical rules (inherited)

- No em-dashes. Use colons, periods, or restructure.
- No emoji.
- No AI attribution in commits or PR bodies.
- Domain-relevant examples where natural: episodes, series, subscriptions, playback, Arabic content, MENA market.

## When in doubt

Read the line out loud as if you were sitting across from a finance director who has fifteen minutes. If it sounds like a resume, rewrite it. If it sounds like a person explaining what now works, ship it.
